In this note I will discuss unbounded weakly harmonic functions on bounded domains. The motivation for this note comes from the study of elliptic regularity theory. In particular, if \(L\) is the divergence form operator \[\begin{equation*} Lu = D_i(a^{ij}(x)D_j u + b^i(x) u) + c^i(x)D_i u + d(x) u, \end{equation*}\] where the coefficients are bounded and the principal coefficients \(a^{ij}\) satisfy the uniform ellipticity condition, then there is a constant \(C>0\) such that for every weak solution \(u\) to \(Lu = g + D_if^i\) in \(\Omega\) that is bounded on \(\partial \Omega\), the estimate \[\begin{equation*} \|u\|_{L^\infty(\Omega)} \leq C(...) \end{equation*}\] holds. This estimate is false in general for solutions \(u\) that are unbounded on \(\partial \Omega\). In this post I will discuss weak solutions of \(\Delta u = 0\) on bounded domains \(\Omega\) that are unbounded on \(\partial \Omega\).

Unbounded Harmonic Functions from the Fundamental Solution

The fundamental solution for \(\Delta\) is \[\begin{equation*} \Gamma(x) = \begin{cases} -\frac 1{2\pi}\log|x| & \text{ if } n = 2\\ \frac1{n(n - 2)\omega_n}|x|^{2- n} & \text{ if }n\geq 3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\] This function is harmonic away from \(x = 0\) and unbounded as \(x\to 0\). Thus, if one is interested in constructing examples of unbounded harmonic functions on bounded domains, the fundamental solution is a natural starting point. The following example is perhaps the simplest example of an unbounded harmonic function on a bounded domain.

Example 1 Let \[\begin{equation*} \Omega = B(e_n, 1) = \{x = (x', x_n) \in \mathbb R^n: |x'|^2 + (x - 1)^2 < 1\}, \end{equation*}\] where \(e_n = (0, \ldots, 0, 1)\) is the \(n^{\text{th}}\) standard basis vector in \(\mathbb R^n\). The fundamental solution \(\Gamma\) is harmonic in \(\Omega\) but unbounded as \(x\to 0\in \partial \Omega\).

When one discusses weak solutions to PDE, one requires that the unknown functions belong to suitable Sobolev spaces. For \(n\geq 3\), the function constructed in Example 1 is not in \(H^1(B(e_n, 1))\). Indeed, for such \(n\), \[\begin{equation*} |D\Gamma(x)|^2 = c_n|x|^{-2(n - 1)} \not\in L^1(B(e_n, 1)), \end{equation*}\] where the failure of integrability is a consequence of the inequality \(2(n - 1)> n\). Thus a natural question is

Is there any bounded domain \(\Omega\subset \mathbb R^n\) for which there exists a weakly harmonic function \(u\in H^1(\Omega)\) that is unbounded on \(\Omega\)?

The answer to this question is “yes” and we can construct such a pair \(\Omega\), \(u\) by modifying the construction of Example 1. In fact, as in Example 1, we will choose \(u = \Gamma\) but now, to achieve the containment \(\Gamma\in H^1(\Omega)\), we simply make \(\partial\Omega\) narrow enough near the singularity so that the desired integrability of \(\Gamma\) and \(D\Gamma\) can be recovered. The following definition quantifies the notion of narrowness that will be used.

Definition 1 (interior $\theta$-singular) For \(\theta\geq 1\), a subset \(\Omega \subset \mathbb R^n\) is said to be interior \(\theta\)-singular at \(x^0\in \partial \Omega\) if there is \(\delta>0\) and a seequence \((x^i)\subset \Omega\) such that \(x^i\to x^0\) and \(B(x^i, \delta|x^i - x^0|^\theta)\subset\Omega\) for all \(i\).

One can easily verify that if \(\Omega\) is interior \(1\)-singular at \(x^0\in \partial \Omega\) then \(\Omega\) is Lipschitz at \(x^0\). Moreover, the standard example of an interior \(\theta\)-singular domain is \[\begin{equation*} \Omega = \{(x', x_n)\in \mathbb R^{n - 1}\times \mathbb R: |x'|< x_n^{1/\theta}\}. \end{equation*}\] In fact, after cutting off the top, this domain will be used in the following example.

Example 2 Let \[ \Omega = \{x = (x', x_n)\in \mathbb R^{n - 1}\times \mathbb R: x_n> |x'|^{1/\theta}\}\cap \{x_n< 1\}. \] The following computation (together with the fact that \(\Gamma\) is less singular at \(0\) than \(D\Gamma\)) shows that for any \(p>1\) one may choose \(\theta\) sufficiently large (depending on \(p\)) so that \(\Gamma\in W^{1, p}(\Omega)\): \[\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \int_\Omega |D\Gamma|^p\; \mathrm d x & \leq c_n\int_0^1\int_{B^{n - 1}(0, x_n^\theta)}(|x'|^2 + x_n^2)^{-\frac{p(n - 1)}2}\; \mathrm d x' \; \mathrm d x_n\\ & \leq c_n\int_0^1 x_n^{-p(n - 1)}|B^{n - 1}(0, x_n^\theta)|\; \mathrm d x_n\\ & \leq c_n \int_0^1 x_n^{-(p- \theta)(n - 1)}\; \mathrm d x_n\\ & \leq C(n, p, \theta) \end{split} \end{equation*}\] provided \(\theta> p - \frac 1 {n - 1}\). In particular, for any \(p>1\), by choosing \(\theta\) as such one finds that \(\Gamma\in W^{1, p}(\Omega)\) is harmonic in \(\Omega\) and unbounded as \(x\to 0\in \partial\Omega\).

Note that for \(p> n\), the construction in Example 2 does not contradict the embedding \(W^{1,p}(\Omega)\hookrightarrow C^{0, 1 - n/p}(\overline\Omega)\) as that embedding requires a smoothness of \(\partial \Omega\) that is not present in the above construction.

Exercise 1 If possible, use the idea of Example 2 to construct an example of a bounded domain \(\Omega\) and a function \(u\in H^1(\Omega)\) for which \(u\) is weakly harmonic in \(\Omega\) and for which \(u\) is unbounded on every ball of the form \(B(x^0, \varepsilon)\) with \(x^0\in \partial \Omega\). I have not tried this exercise but, if I were to try it, I would attempt to use a limiting procedure to construct \(\Omega\) that is interior \(\theta\)-singular on a countable dense subset of \(\{x^j:j\in \mathbb N\}\subset \partial \Omega\) and then construct \(u\) as a weighted sum of the form \(u = \sum_{j = 1}^\infty w_j\Gamma(x - x^j)\), where \(\{w_j\}_{j = 1}^\infty\subset \mathbb R\) are suitably chosen weights.

In view of the constructions in Examples 1 and 2, one is naturally led to ask the following question:

Is there any smooth bounded domain \(\Omega\subset \mathbb R^n\) together with a weakly harmonic function \(u\in H^1(\Omega)\) for which \(u\) is unbounded near \(\partial \Omega\)?

Unbounded Weakly Harmonic Functions on Smooth Bounded Domains

Definition 2 (Fractional Sobolev Spaces) For \(0< s < 1\) and \(\Omega \subset \mathbb R^n\), the (\(L^2\)-based) fractional Sobolev space of order \(s\) is denoted \(H^s(\Omega)\) and is defined by \[\begin{equation*} H^s(\Omega) = \{u\in \mathcal D'(\Omega): \|u\|_{H^s(\Omega)}< \infty\}, \end{equation*}\] where \[\begin{equation*} \|u\|_{H^s(\Omega)}^2 = \|u\|_{L^2(\Omega)}^2 + \int_\Omega\int_\Omega\frac{|u(x) - u(y)|^2}{|x- y|^{n + 2s}}\; \mathrm d x \; \mathrm d y. \end{equation*}\]

Let us consider the problem \[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \Delta u = 0 & \text{ on }B_1\\ u= g & \text{ on }\partial B_1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\] where \(g\in H^{1/2}(\partial B_1)\). The trace theorem ensures that \(H^{1/2}(\partial B_1)\) is the space of traces of \(H^1(B_1)\) functions. That is, if \(\gamma_0\) is the trace map then \(H^{1/2}(\partial B_1) = \gamma_0(H^1(B_1))\). Thus, given \(g\in H^{1/2}(\partial B_1)\) there is \(v\in H^1(B_1)\) such that \(\gamma_0(v) = g\). Let us fix any such \(v\). Since \(\Delta v\in H^{-1}(B_1)\), the Lax-Milgram Theorem guarantees that the problem \[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} -\Delta w = \Delta v & \text{ in }B_1\\ w = 0 & \text{ on }\partial B_1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\] has a unique solution \(w\in H^1(B_1)\) and that \[\begin{equation*} \|w\|_{H^1(B_1)} \leq C\|\Delta v\|_{H^{-1}(B_1)} \leq C\|v\|_{H^1(B_1)}. \end{equation*}\] Now fixing such \(w\) and defining \(u\in H^1(B_1)\) by \(u= v + w\) we find that \(u\) satisfies \[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \Delta u= 0 & \text{ in }B_1\\ u = g & \text{ on }\partial B_1. \end{cases} \end{equation*}\] Moreover, \[\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \|u\|_{H^1(B_1)} & \leq \|v\|_{H^1(B_1)} + \|w\|_{H^1(B_1)}\\ & \leq C\|v\|_{H^1(B_1)}\\ &\leq C\|g\|_{H^{1/2}(B_1)}, \end{split} \end{equation*}\] where the final inequality follows from the fact that \(\gamma_0:H^1(B_1)\to H^{1/2}(\partial B_1)\) is a bounded linear surjectiv emap, so the Open Mapping Theorem ensures that \(\gamma_0\) is bounded below by a positve constant.
